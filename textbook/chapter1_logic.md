# The National Textbook Project

By issuing the textbooks for the third year of each educational cycle, the Educational Center for Research and Development will have completed the third and last installment of books called for by the New Curricula. We are placing these books in the hands of students with the great hope that we are moving, step by step, toward the goal of acquiring sound learning, using sophisticated educational means and up-to-date methodology that encourage and reinforce individual thinking and research, acquisition of skills, development of ethical and national attitudes, the feeling of national belonging as well as the feeling of belonging to humanity at large.

The on-going revolution in information, communication and educational-aids technology has undoubtedly limited the role of the textbook and lowered the rank it used to occupy. However, in our society and in many other societies, the textbook remains the basic means of education, and it is our duty to exert our utmost effort and care to come up with the best product as to form and content. Yet we should not lose sight of the fact that the textbook is not sufficient by itself but should rather be used as a stepping stone to access other sources of information. What is important is to keep a clear vision and maintain the right course toward our objective. The means should not turn into the end and the student should always remain the focus of the learning/teaching process.

No one ignores or denies the fact that textbook writing requires very high academic and educational qualifications and very wide field experience. The authors committees undeniably possess such qualities. Yet the textbooks of the last two years contained some negative aspects. Such is the nature of human work, no matter how good the intentions or how great the effort extended. Here constructive criticism constitutes a real contribution to raising the standard of authorship, minimizing errors and filling gaps. We say that, with all due appreciation and respect to all those who have contributed to the success of this project.

The Educational Center for Research and Development is embarking this year on a process of evaluating the New Curricula and related textbooks, teacher training courses and student achievement. This is a natural and necessary step now that the new system has been put into effect. This process aims at identifying the curricular objectives that have been achieved as well as those that have not been achieved, with a view to proceeding with the positive aspects and correcting the negative ones.

As part of this correction process, we plan to review the versions that have been issued in order to secure good textbooks for our students, who always deserve the best.

March 13, 2000

**President, Educational Center for Research and Development**

**Nemer FRAYHA**

# PREFACE

The third year of secondary education is the last year in this stage. The mathematics book for "The General Sciences" consists of two parts: a part that deals with themes related to algebra and geometry, and a part that deals with calculus, probability, and statistics. The authors decided to dedicate a book to each part with the view that this division, although not quite realistic in mathematics, will make the book easier and more practical for the student and teacher to work with.

The two books continue to follow the same pattern as the other books. They were created and edited in compliance with the demands of the new program. Here we find: the scientific content, unambiguously detailed and defined; the targeted objectives, controlled and evaluated; and the explicity shown method recommended.

Far from being a dry account of facts, this work is a tool that permits and promotes the active participation of learners. We wanted to create an instructive product suitable for an education based on the balanced and well-defined division of labor between three parties: the student - the instructor - the administrator.

The instructor is not only the possessor of knowledge but is more the leader of the class; he/she guides it in its choice of orientations, maintains its interest, promotes research among its members, and helps find results while indicating their applications.

At the heart of the educative system is the student, a person with the right to an education worthy of a free individual, independent and seeking knowledge. It is the student who, without feeling forced to put up with the knowledge he/she must learn, should feel constantly faced with situations that stimulate his/her desire to know more.

- In what concerns new notions (such as logic) that assume that the student has a high level of maturity, the appeal will be to his/her intuition and not to his/her reasoning.
- Pleasant and easy proofs were kept in the text while those that seemed to us complicated and difficult were omitted. Our objective was to introduce the student to reasoning and to prepare him/her to tackle the exercises at the end of the chapter with ease.
- The "Just for Fun" heading closes each chapter of the book. This will be the focus of a weekly session reserved for independent mathematical research with a theme (that the teacher will choose or modify according to the level of the class). Some may think that this approach is "not classical" and so oppose it by naming it a "waste of time." We reply, however, that the benefits of such research by far compensate for the relative length of time allocated to it.
- This year, a new heading appears in some chapters. It is the heading "Selected Reading." It presents the student with historical notes and useful comments.

Finally, any remarks, criticisms, or suggestions that colleagues would like to make will be received with attention.

*The authors*

# 1 — LOGIC

## INTRODUCTION

Man's interest in logic goes back to ancient times. The Greeks were the first to give this branch of knowledge its place of honor. Logic is found at the very base of reasoning. Unfortunately however, the intuitive manipulation of this discipline, that deals with statements, may lead to contradictory results. Hence the necessity to introduce specific rules. For a long time handling these rules remained difficult and arduous, but the development of algebra allowed a relatively simple presentation of logic. The basic idea is to consider a statement as a variable that may have one value of two : $T$ (if true) and $F$ (if false).

Formal logic is only used in mathematics; it is widely used in concepts of electronic circuits, called **logic circuits**, which are used to manufacture many electronic machines (particularly computers).

This chapter intends to :

1. specify the notion of statements;
2. state the rules that guide the use of logical connectives;
3. explicitly explain the fundamental rules of logic.

## TEXT

### I — STATEMENTS

In logic, a **statement** is a sentence characterized by the fact that it is either true or false.

— *Examples* —

1. "Beirut is an Asian city" is a statement since it is a true sentence.
2. "Every isosceles triangle is right" is a false statement.
3. "What time is it ?" is not a statement.

### II — TRUTH VALUES

To each statement $p$, assign the letter $T$ if it is true and the letter $F$ if it is false. The letter $T$ or $F$ is called the **truth value** of the statement $p$.

> $F$ is the **truth value** of the statement «$\pi$ is a rationnel number».

The truth value of a statement complies with the following two principles :

**The principle of contradiction :** A statement cannot be true and false at the same time.

**The principle of excluded middle :** A statement is either true or false and nothing else.

The preceding text enables us to think of a statement as a variable that takes two values in the set $\{T, F\}$. A statement is defined when its truth value is stated. Thus, it is possible to create new statements from others by defining their truth values in terms of those statements that form them.

> To calculate the truth value of a composite statement, we use a table called the **truth table**.

> The truth table of the statement $p$ **or** (not $q$) is the following:
>
> | $p$ | $q$ | not $p$ | $q$ or (not $q$) |
> |---|---|---|---|
> | $T$ | $T$ | $F$ | $T$ |
> | $T$ | $F$ | $F$ | $F$ |
> | $F$ | $T$ | $T$ | $T$ |
> | $F$ | $F$ | $T$ | $T$ |

A statement, formed of other statements, is a **tautology** if it is true whatever the truth values of the statements from which it is formed. Similarly, a statement is a **contradiction** if it is false for any truth values of the statements from which it is formed.

— *Example* —

If $p$ is a statement, then $p$ or (not $p$) is a tautology. On the other hand, the statement $p$ and (not $p$) is a contradiction.

### III — NEGATION OF A STATEMENT

Let $p$ be a statement. We can construct, from $p$, a new statement called the **negation** of $p$ and that is denoted $\neg p$ (read *not p*). Its truth value is defined by the following rule:

$$\neg p \text{ is true if and only if } p \text{ is false.}$$

| $p$ | $\neg p$ |
|---|---|
| $T$ | $F$ |
| $F$ | $T$ |

> What is the negation of the statement "$x < 3$?"

— *Example* —

The negation of the statement "All Martians are green" is the statement "There exists at least one Martian who is not green."

### IV — LOGICAL CONNECTIVES

> We use these connectives to create new statements from other statements.

In making calculations, numbers and variables are combined by means of arithmetical operations such as addition and multiplication. In logic, there also exist connectives to combine statements. In this book, the basic connectives are four. They are the following:

a) **The conjunction AND is symbolized by $\wedge$** : If $p$ and $q$ are two statements, the statement $p \wedge q$ (read $p$ **and** $q$) is the statement which is true if and only if $p$ and $q$ are simultaneously true, and false in the other cases.

| $p$ | $q$ | $p \wedge q$ |
|---|---|---|
| $T$ | $T$ | $T$ |
| $T$ | $F$ | $F$ |
| $F$ | $T$ | $F$ |
| $F$ | $F$ | $F$ |

> This statement is true.

— *Example* —

"A square is a rectangle and a rhombus" is the statement obtained by combining the following two statements by AND: "A square is a rectangle" and "A square is a rhombus."

b) **The disjunction OR is symbolized by $\vee$** : If $p$ and $q$ are two statements, the statement $p \vee q$ (read $p$ **or** $q$) is the statement which is false if and only if $p$ and $q$ are simultaneously false and true for other cases.

| $p$ | $q$ | $p \vee q$ |
|---|---|---|
| $T$ | $T$ | $T$ |
| $T$ | $F$ | $T$ |
| $F$ | $T$ | $T$ |
| $F$ | $F$ | $F$ |

— *Example* —

The statement "Salah loves Rana or Mona" is the statement obtained by combining the following two statements : "Salah loves Rana" and "Salah loves Mona."

> Can Salah love Rana and Mona at the same time?

— *Remark* —

The connective "OR" is confusing. The sentence "A nonzero natural number $x$ is either even or odd" is interpreted literally as excluding the possibility that $x$ can be both. The connective "OR" as defined in logic does not exclude this possibility, i.e. at least one of the two alternatives occurs. Hence comes the necessity to understand the logical "OR."

> The statement "Triangle $ABC$ is right or isosceles" does not exclude the fact that $ABC$ may be right and isosceles at the same time.

c) **The conditional IF... THEN... is symbolized by $\Rightarrow$.** If $p$ and $q$ are two statements, the statement $(\neg p) \vee q$ is denoted by $p \Rightarrow q$ (read « **IF** $p$ **THEN** $q$ » or « $p$ **implies** $q$ »). The connective is false if and only if p is true and q is false at the same time. It is true in all other cases.

| $p$ | $q$ | $p \Rightarrow q$ |
|---|---|---|
| $T$ | $T$ | $T$ |
| $T$ | $F$ | $F$ |
| $F$ | $T$ | $T$ |
| $F$ | $F$ | $T$ |

The definition of $p \Rightarrow q$ gives this statement the value 'true' if $p$ is false, and this, for any truth value of $q$. As a result, 'false' implies anything.

— *Example* —

"If $2 = 3$, then $20 = 21$" is a statement.

> This statement is true.

— *Remark* —

If $p \Rightarrow q$ is true and if $p$ is true, then $q$ is true. The truth of $p \Rightarrow q$ alone is not enough to deduce that $q$ is true.

d) **The biconditional IF AND ONLY IF is symbolized by $\Leftrightarrow$.** If $p$ and $q$ are two statements, the statement $(p \Rightarrow q) \wedge (q \Rightarrow p)$ is denoted by $p \Leftrightarrow q$ (read « $p$ **IF AND ONLY IF** $q$ » or « $p$ **is equivalent to** $q$ »). It is true if and only if $p$ and $q$ have the same truth value, i.e., if the two statements are true or false at the same time. It is false in the other cases.

| $p$ | $q$ | $p \Leftrightarrow q$ |
|---|---|---|
| $T$ | $T$ | $T$ |
| $T$ | $F$ | $F$ |
| $F$ | $T$ | $F$ |
| $F$ | $F$ | $T$ |

— *Example* —

"The angles of a quadrilateral are equal if and only if its sides are equal" is a false statement. Similarly, "The angles of a triangle are equal if and only if its sides are equal" is a true statement.

### V — FUNDAMENTAL RULES

The following statements are tautologies. They represent the fundamental rules used in proofs.

1. $(\neg \neg p) \Leftrightarrow p$
2. $[p \wedge (p \Rightarrow q)] \Rightarrow q$
3. $[\neg(p \wedge q)] \Leftrightarrow [(\neg p) \vee (\neg q)]$
4. $[\neg(p \vee q)] \Leftrightarrow [(\neg p) \wedge (\neg q)]$
5. $[(p \Rightarrow q) \wedge (q \Rightarrow r)] \Rightarrow [p \Rightarrow r]$
6. $[p \Rightarrow q] \Leftrightarrow [(\neg q) \Rightarrow (\neg p)]$

## FOCUS

1. A statement is a sentence that is either true or false.
2. A statement cannot be true and false at the same time.
3. The truth value of a composite statement can be determined from the values of its components by applying the following rules :

| The composite statement | is true when | is false when |
|---|---|---|
| $\neg p$ | $p$ is false | $p$ is true |
| $p \wedge q$ | $p$ and $q$ are true at the same time | at least one of the two is false |
| $p \vee q$ | at least one of the two statements is true | the two statements are false at the same time |
| $p \Rightarrow q$ | $p$ is false or $q$ is true | $p$ is true and $q$ is false |
| $p \Leftrightarrow q$ | $p$ and $q$ are true at the same time or false at the same time | one of the two statements is true and the other is false at the same time |
